Tech Thoughts

This page is a little different from a normal “review site.” I sat down and tried to list every gadget, screen, and gizmo I have actually used in my life, in rough order, from the time I was a kid on Windows XP to the smart-stuff in my room today. It is not scientific—it is memory plus honesty. I am not here to flex; I am here to show how a random stack of hardware shapes what you get used to.

Under each item I wrote my take first, then a short “What you usually read online” blurb. For a few products I poked at common reviewer and forum themes (headphones, phones, and anything where the internet has strong opinions). For the rest, the second paragraph is the kind of generic public chatter you see—useful for context, not a replacement for a full review. If I got something wrong, that is on me, not a lab.

Windows XP — my first “real” PC era

I was so young that I do not have a fair, grown-up “review” of Windows XP. What I do remember is a lot of Grand Theft Auto: Vice City, San Andreas, Plants vs. Zombies, WWE Raw vs. SmackDown, Project IGI, Hitman, Counter-Strike, and Call of Duty (1 or 2— the memories blur), plus a pile of other titles I probably forgot to name. Basically: the PC was a game launcher and I was happy.

What you usually read online (nostalgia edition): XP is frozen in time as the OS that “just worked” for a whole generation. People today run it in VMs for fun; security-wise it is ancient history. For me it was not “how stable was the kernel”—it was “how fast can I load the next level.”

Nokia “brick” phone

The classic indestructible Nokia. I can’t add deep thoughts—it was a phone, it called people, it survived being dropped, end of story. Solid. Reliable. No drama.

What you usually read online: The meme is true: pre-smartphone Nokias are basically civilization-scale hammers. Forums still joke that one could survive the heat death of the universe.

Lenovo school tablet

We used a Lenovo tablet in school for “education,” and, honestly, the experience was rough—slow, clunky, the kind of device that made you miss paper. I will leave it at that.

What you usually read online: Budget education tablets are often underpowered, locked down, and loaded with software the hardware can barely run—exactly the kind of thing Reddit threads grumble about when a district picks the cheapest option.

iPhone 5 — my first phone

I loved this phone. I was still a kid, so a huge chunk of my time went into mobile games: Jetpack Joyride, Angry Birds, the whole casual-game rotation—stuff like Road Warrior, Hill Climb (you know the one), Perfect Cell, and whatever else the App Store fed me. It felt like holding the future in my hand after the Nokia and the cursed school tablet.

What you usually read online: The iPhone 5 is remembered as a huge leap in thin, light design for its time, but with an aging 4" screen and dying battery stories today. Back then, for a lot of people, it was the perfect upgrade from a dumb phone.

PlayStation 3 (at a friend’s place)

I did not own a PS3, but I logged serious hours at a friend’s house. A lot of good memories on that thing. I played Batman: Arkham City for the first time there, and that game basically rewired my brain for what a “good” action game could feel like.

What you usually read online: The PS3 era is a nostalgia goldmine: online services had quirks, the Cell processor was a weird beast for devs, but the library of exclusives and third-party hits still makes “PS3 in 20xx” threads pop up in gaming subs.

PlayStation 4 — and Arkham Knight

When we got a PS4, it came with Batman: Arkham Knight—and that game cranked up my love for gaming. These days I put a lot of hours into FIFA / EA FC; the PS4 is where a lot of that “keep playing, one more match” habit really stuck.

What you usually read online: PS4 is often called one of the best console generations for sheer library size; Arkham Knight had a rough PC launch but is still praised on console. Sports-game fans, meanwhile, debate EA every single year. Same as it ever was.

Sony Bravia 55" — the living-room TV in India

We had a Sony Bravia 55-inch when that was basically luxury in India. It was expensive, kind of overpriced, but it was also one of the first big Android TV experiences in the market there at the time. Having “smart” baked in felt like a flex even when the software was half-baked in the early days.

What you usually read online: Sony’s picture quality usually gets a nod in TV roundups, while smart-TV software gets mixed reviews (slow apps, update roulette). The “is this overpriced for India?” question still shows up in local forums whenever new Bravias drop.

Sony WH-CH710N — my first real headphones

My first over-ear wireless pair. They were a big deal to me: noise cancelling on a walk to class, music without bothering anyone. I was not an audiophile; I was just happy to have a proper pair.

What you usually read online (from reviews and Reddit): These are often called a value pick: decent ANC for the money, long battery life (often quoted in the 30+ hour range), and very light on the head. The tradeoffs are plasticky build, so-so sound detail compared to Sony’s 1000X line, and ANC that can’t match the flagship models—the sort of “good enough” summary you see on SoundGuys– style writeups and in “budget ANC” threads. That matches my “first headphones, not a flex” vibe.

ASUS ROG Strix G17 (2022) laptop

My gaming / school laptop chapter. A big 17" ROG Strix meant I could actually run the games I cared about and still drag the thing to campus when I had to. It was not ultraportable, but it was powerful.

What you usually read online: ROG Strix lines are a staple in “best gaming laptop” searches: strong GPUs for the class, loud fans under load, and a chunky body you feel in your backpack. Reviewers and buyers often repeat the same mantra: great for performance, not for pretending you own an ultrabook.

OnePlus 11 — first phone I bought with my own money

The OnePlus 11 is the first device I paid for entirely with my own money. Every gadget below it on this page (Echo, DualSense, watch, TV, my PC, smart fan, camera, lights—the whole stack) is in the same boat. Everything above it was family, school, or life handing me hardware, not my bank account choosing. That line in the list matters to me more than it probably should.

I picked OnePlus for the usual reasons people do: it felt fast, the charging was nuts in a good way, and I did not want to pay full Samsung-Apple tax if I could avoid it. It is my daily driver brain.

What you usually read online (CNET, PCMag, Reddit, long-term threads): Reviewers praise the Snapdragon 8 Gen 2 performance and wired fast charging on the OnePlus 11 and call it a strong value. The recurring knocks: no wireless charging (for some people that is a dealbreaker), cameras that are good but not class-leading, and software that feels a bit more like Oppo/ColorOS than the old “pure” OnePlus days. I still like the phone, but the internet and I can agree: it is a flagship-style tradeoff, not a halo phone for everyone.

Amazon Echo (4th gen)

I jumped into the smart speaker world with a 4th-gen Echo. It is handy for timers, music, and controlling other stuff—more on that in a second.

What you usually read online: Echo devices win on Alexa ecosystem depth and price; privacy-focused folks still side-eye always-on mics, while convenience-focused people fill threads with “here are my favorite routines.”

DualSense (PS5 controller) — and stick drift

I love the DualSense—haptics, feel, the whole “next-gen” thing—until stick drift showed up, which is as annoying as everyone says. I went a little extra and bought TMR joysticks, then had a repair person swap them in so I could stop fighting the hardware. No regrets; drifting sticks are a bad final boss in real life.

What you usually read online: Drift is one of the most complained-about issues for modern gamepads, PlayStation included. The mod community talks about Hall effect / TMR sticks as longer-lasting fixes. My story is basically a forum post with a budget and a happy ending.

OnePlus Watch 3

A wearable on the same ecosystem vibe as the phone. I use it the way most people do: fitness stuff, notifications, and pretending I have my life together.

What you usually read online: Non-Apple smartwatches often get compared to the Apple Watch on day one and then judged on battery life, health accuracy, and app polish. The tone is usually “close enough for Android” or “wait for a sale.”

Toshiba Fire TV 42"

A smaller Fire TV for a desk or a bedroom screen—cheap, fine, and very Amazon first. I use it the way you’d expect: streaming, and not thinking too hard about it.

What you usually read online: Budget Fire TVs trade picture quality and brightness for “this cost how little?” People either love the value or get picky about color accuracy—there is no middle ground in the comments.

My custom PC (the main character)

The desktop I built is the heart of the setup now. You can read the full story and parts list on the My Journey page. Short version: it is the tool I use for CS work, games, and everything heavy.

What you usually read online: The PC world never agrees on one “best” build, but people always agree you should not cheap out on the power supply and you should match the CPU, board, and RAM to what you actually do. I tried to do exactly that.

Dreo smart room fan + smart lights + Roku camera

I rounded out my room with a Dreo smart fan (airflow you can script like a nerd), smart lights for a small Alexa ecosystem, and a Roku indoor camera because as an international student I do not want to take chances with safety—I would rather have a little extra peace of mind than “maybe it will be fine.”

What you usually read online: Smart home threads split between “I love automations” and “why did this firmware update break my kitchen light.” For cameras, the debate is always privacy vs. security vs. cost; for fans and bulbs, it is “does it integrate with my voice assistant.” I am right in the middle of that Venn diagram.

Closing take

If you read this far, you have basically seen the shape of my tech life: early PC games, durable phones, a few bad school gadgets, a long console phase, a living-room screen that felt huge at home, headphones and laptops for college, then phones and gear I paid for myself, and finally a room that talks back and a PC that can carry my degree. None of it is a flex list—it is just context for how I think about tech now: I care about stuff that lasts, does not fight me, and actually fits a student life.

I will keep updating this as I get older and the list gets longer. If you disagree with an internet “consensus” blurb, same—I am only summarizing the vibe of what people type in reviews, not the truth from on high.
